Hedy Lamarr

Often called "The Most Beautiful Woman in Film," Hedy Lamarr's beauty and screen presence made her one of the most popular actresses of her day.

She was born Hedwig Eva Maria Kiesler on November 9, 1914 in Vienna, Austria. At 17 years old, Hedy starred in her first film, a German project called Geld auf der Strase. The 1932 film Exstase brought her to the attention of Hollywood producers.

Once in Hollywood, she changed her name to Hedy Lamarr and starred in her first Hollywood film, Algiers (1938). She continued to act opposite stars such as Spencer Tracy, Clark Gable and Jimmy Stewart.

Among her notable films are adaptations of John Steinbeck's Tortilla Flat (1942), White Cargo (1942), Cecil B. DeMille's Samson and Delilah (1949) and The Female Animal (1957).

Inventor

Beyond the screen, Lamarr co-invented a technology known as frequency-hopping spread spectrum.

This system was designed to avoid the interception of radio signals by constantly switching frequencies. The idea came during World War II and was meant to make torpedo control signals immune to jamming. Hedy collaborated with composer George Antheil, using the concept of piano rolls to synchronize signal jumps. The technique was later patented in 1942 as "Secret Communication System" (US Patent 2,292,387).
